I became a student at Lock College, and although I preferred to remain low-key, the arrogant, third-rate villains at the school wouldn’t let me.

Lock College was exactly how I wrote it—too grand for an academy and too dangerous to be called a school. The gates alone were designed to intimidate: towering iron structures etched with runes that pulsed faintly, scanning anyone who passed through. I remembered adding that detail to keep “unworthy characters” out.

Now I wasn’t so sure I qualified as worthy.

Students filled the courtyard, most of them already divided into the same cliques I had outlined in my drafts—the elites, the opportunists, and the ones pretending to be harmless. I kept my head down, adjusting the plain uniform I’d deliberately chosen. No insignias. No family crest. Nothing that would attract attention.

That was the plan.

It lasted less than five minutes.

“Hey. You.”

I didn’t respond. In stories, that’s usually how background characters survive—by not engaging.

A hand grabbed my shoulder.

Unfortunate.

I turned slowly to face them. Three of them. Just like I’d written—overconfident, poorly developed antagonists meant to fill space before the real threats appeared. I even remembered giving one of them a scar across his eyebrow to make him look more “menacing.”

Up close, it just looked forced.

“You’re new,” the scarred one said, looking me up and down. “No crest. No rank. You trying to hide something?”

Yes. Everything.

But I shook my head. “Just trying to get to class.”

He smirked, glancing at his friends. “Hear that? He thinks he can just walk around like he belongs here.”

I sighed internally.

This was the problem with writing shallow villains. They don’t know when to stop.

“I really don’t have time for this,” I said, stepping slightly to the side.

That was apparently the wrong move.

His expression hardened. “You don’t get to ignore me.”

Of course I don’t, I thought. Because I wrote you that way.

He reached out again, but this time I caught his wrist.

That surprised both of us.

I paused.

That… wasn’t supposed to happen.

In my notes, I wasn’t strong. Not here. Not at this stage. I was just an observer—someone who understood the world, not someone who could fight in it.

So why did that feel so easy?

The courtyard went quiet around us.

“Let go,” he said, his voice lower now.

I did.

Not because he told me to—but because something felt off.

Very off.

I took a step back, studying my own hand.

“If I’m not following the script…” I muttered under my breath, “…then what exactly am I?”

The scarred student hesitated now, clearly unsure whether to keep pushing.

Good. That part, at least, I hadn’t planned.

I adjusted my sleeve and looked at them again, this time without avoiding eye contact.

“I said I’m just trying to get to class,” I repeated.

There was a brief silence.

Then one of them scoffed, pulling the scarred one back. “Forget it. Not worth it.”

They walked off, but not without throwing one last glare over their shoulders.

I exhaled slowly.

That could’ve gone worse.

But also… it shouldn’t have gone that well.

As I continued toward the main building, one thought settled in:

This world wasn’t following my story anymore.

And if the rules were changing…

Then even the things I thought I understood could get me killed.
