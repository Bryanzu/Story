I woke up, only to find myself in an entirely new world.

At first, I thought it was a dream—the kind that feels too real but unravels the moment you focus on the details. But the air had weight. The wind carried a strange metallic scent. And when I pushed myself up from the ground, the dirt under my fingers felt rough, dry… real.

Then I saw it.

The skyline.

Jagged towers of black stone stretched into a sky that wasn’t quite blue, streaked with faint glowing lines like cracks in glass. I knew that place. Every inch of it.

My stomach dropped.

“I know this…” I muttered.

And that’s when it hit me.

This wasn’t just any world.

This was *my* world.

Not mine as in where I lived—but mine as in what I created. The world from the novel I’d been writing for the past two years. The same one I abandoned halfway because I couldn’t figure out how to fix the plot.

“...You’ve got to be kidding me.”

I stood up slowly, heart starting to race. If this really was my story, then—

A distant roar echoed across the land.

Not just any roar.

*That* roar.

I froze.

“No. No, no, no…”

I knew exactly what made that sound. I wrote it. Described it in detail. The scale, the fire, the way it hunted—

“Why did I make it so strong?” I whispered.

A shadow moved across the ground.

I didn’t look up immediately. I didn’t want to confirm it.

But I had to.

Slowly, I lifted my head.

And there it was.

Massive. Winged. Burning with the same crimson glow I had once proudly described as “unstoppable.”

Now it was very stoppable.

Preferably by someone else.

“Okay,” I said, taking a step back. “Think. Think.”

If I was in my novel, then the rules still applied. The magic system. The characters. The events.

Which meant—

My eyes widened.

“The main character…”

If the story was still on track, then somewhere out there, the protagonist—the one I designed to actually survive all this—was alive.

And if I could find them…

I might not die in the first chapter of my own story.

Another roar.

Closer this time.

“Right,” I said, turning and starting to run. “Step one: don’t get eaten.”

As I sprinted across the cracked ground, one thought kept repeating in my head:

I didn’t just write this world.

I made it dangerous.

And now I had to survive it.
